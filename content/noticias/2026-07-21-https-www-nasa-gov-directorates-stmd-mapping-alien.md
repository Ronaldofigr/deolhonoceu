To address that problem, the project proposes a new type of nulling interferometer called “dynamic hierarchical nulling.” An interferometer combines light received through different inputs in a carefully controlled way. In nulling, that combination is arranged to suppress unwanted starlight, making the much fainter planetary signal easier to isolate. “Hierarchical” indicates that the inputs would be combined in stages, while “dynamic” points to an arrangement that can be adjusted as observations are made.

The second part of the proposed innovation is connected to optical very long baseline interferometry, or optical VLBI. In simple terms, interferometry allows separated instruments or inputs to contribute to a single reconstruction, providing access to details that would not be distinguished in the same way by one observer alone. Doing this with visible light requires exceptionally careful handling of how the optical signals are brought together.

If the proposed approach can be developed, it could help move exoplanet research toward a more detailed form of observation: from detecting worlds and measuring their overall properties to reconstructing images that contain spatial variations.
